## SECTORAL COMPOSITION (Figures 1-4)

### Figure 1: Kazakhstan's Exports to China by Sector, 2015-2024

**Caption:**  
Kazakhstan's exports to China are dominated by raw materials and primary products. Energy (crude oil, natural gas) consistently represents 40-55% of total exports, while metals and minerals (copper, iron, zinc, rare earths) account for another 30-45%. Agriculture, chemicals, and other products make up the remainder. Total exports grew from ~$8.4 billion (2015) to ~$18.5 billion (2024), representing a 120% increase driven primarily by commodity price fluctuations and volume growth.

**Data:**

- Stacked area chart showing annual export values by HS 2-digit sector
- Five major sectors shown: Energy (HS 27), Metals & Minerals (HS 26, 71-83), Agriculture (HS 1-24), Chemicals (HS 28-38), and Other products
- Values in millions USD

**Interpretation:**  
Kazakhstan's export structure reveals commodity-dependent trade with China. The lack of diversification into manufactured goods (machinery, vehicles) demonstrates Kazakhstan's position as a resource supplier rather than a value-added producer. The post-2020 export growth reflects post-COVID normalization and commodity price recovery, not structural diversification.

**Publication Note:**  
"Figure 1 illustrates the sectoral composition of Kazakhstan's exports to China from 2015-2024, showing the persistent dominance of energy and metal exports."

### Figure 2: Kazakhstan's Imports from China by Sector, 2015-2024

**Caption:**  
Kazakhstan's imports from China exhibit a starkly different pattern from exports, dominated by manufactured goods. Machinery and electrical equipment (HS 84-85) consistently represent 25-35% of imports, while vehicles (HS 87) account for 15-25%, and textiles (HS 50-63) for 10-18%. These three categories alone comprise 50-80% of total imports from China. Total imports surged from ~$6.2 billion (2015) to ~$22.5 billion (2024), a 263% increase—nearly 2.3 times faster than export growth.

**Data:**

- Stacked area chart showing annual import values by HS 2-digit sector
- Seven major sectors shown: Machinery & Electrical, Vehicles, Textiles, Chemicals, Plastics & Rubber, Metals & Minerals, and Other products
- Values in millions USD

**Interpretation:**  
The import surge reflects Kazakhstan's consumption of Chinese manufactured goods and its limited domestic manufacturing capacity. The 263% import growth versus 120% export growth is the primary driver of the 2023 trade deficit—Kazakhstan is importing substantially more than it is exporting. This pattern suggests post-COVID demand recovery and increased consumer/industrial reliance on Chinese products.

**Publication Note:**  
"Figure 2 shows Kazakhstan's imports from China are increasingly concentrated in machinery, vehicles, and textiles, with total import growth substantially outpacing export growth."

### Figure 3: Kazakhstan-China Trade Balance by Major Sector, 2015-2024

**Caption:**  
The bilateral trade balance shifted dramatically in 2023, driven by deterioration in multiple sectors. Energy maintains a large and consistent surplus (+$8-12 billion annually), reflecting Kazakhstan's dominant position as a hydrocarbon exporter. Metals and minerals also show persistent surpluses (+$3-6 billion). However, machinery and electrical products have shifted from modest surpluses to deficits of -$2 to -$4 billion annually, while vehicles changed from near-balance to deficits exceeding -$2 billion. This sectoral deterioration explains the aggregate transition from a +$13.8 billion surplus in 2022 to a -$4.0 billion deficit in 2023.

**Data:**

- Connected line chart with four major sectors tracked separately
- Energy (navy), Metals & Minerals (red), Machinery & Electrical (green), Vehicles (purple)
- Horizontal zero line marks the balance point (surplus above, deficit below)
- Values in millions USD

**Interpretation:**  
This figure identifies the specific sectors driving the 2023 inflection point. The energy and metals surpluses depend on commodity prices and production volumes, while the widening deficits in machinery and vehicles reflect structural consumption patterns—Kazakhstan increasingly imports finished goods that it does not produce domestically.

**Publication Note:**  
"The sectoral balance analysis (Figure 3) reveals that while energy and metals exports maintain strong surpluses, escalating machinery and vehicle imports drive the aggregate trade deficit reversal in 2023."

### Figure 4: China's Import Penetration by Sector, 2015-2024

**Caption:**  
China accounts for exceptionally high shares of Kazakhstan's imports in key sectors. By 2023, China supplied 77.3% of all machinery and electrical equipment imports, 84.5% of vehicles, 89.8% of textiles, 47.2% of chemicals, and 52.1% of plastics and rubber. These penetration rates have grown throughout the decade, indicating increasing supply chain concentration and rising dependence on Chinese producers. The 2020-2022 dip reflects temporary COVID-related trade disruptions, but penetration rebounded sharply post-2022.

**Data:**

- Connected line chart showing China's share of total imports in each sector (0-50% scale)
- Five sectors: Machinery & Electrical, Vehicles, Textiles, Chemicals, Plastics & Rubber
- Penetration calculated as: (China imports / Total sector imports) × 100

**Interpretation:**  
The extreme penetration rates in textiles (89.8%), vehicles (84.5%), and machinery (77.3%) indicate that Kazakhstan has become highly dependent on Chinese sources for manufactured goods. This dependence creates potential supply chain vulnerabilities and suggests limited domestic manufacturing capacity or competitiveness. The consistent upward trend across all sectors indicates structural deepening of bilateral trade ties, not cyclical variation.

**Publication Note:**  
"China's market penetration (Figure 4) demonstrates overwhelming dominance in machinery, vehicles, and textiles, with penetration rates reaching 77-90% by 2023."

### Figure 6: Trade Concentration Index by Sector, 2015-2024

**Caption:**  
The concentration index measures the degree to which Kazakhstan's bilateral trade with China is concentrated in specific sectors. Higher values indicate more concentrated trade (few dominant sectors). The index has remained relatively stable between 0.25-0.35 throughout the decade, indicating moderately high concentration. Energy and metals consistently dominate both exports and imports. This moderate-to-high concentration level suggests that the bilateral relationship remains commodity-centric with limited sectoral diversification.

**Data:**

- Time series of Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) calculated annually
- HHI = Σ(sector_share)² where sector_share = sectoral_trade_value / total_trade_value
- Index ranges from 0 (perfect diversification) to 1 (complete concentration in one sector)
- Annual observations from 2015-2024

**Interpretation:**  
The stable concentration index suggests the trade relationship has not materially diversified. While growth has occurred across sectors, relative proportions have remained fairly constant. The lack of declining concentration indicates no fundamental shift toward higher-value manufactured trade; instead, growth has been proportional across existing sectors.

**Publication Note:**  
"The concentration index (Figure 6) reveals a structurally stable bilateral relationship, with persistent dominance of energy and metals exports."

### Figure 7: Trade Growth Asymmetry Index, 2015-2024

**Caption:**  
The asymmetry index measures the relative growth rates of Kazakhstan's exports versus imports from China. Values above 1.0 indicate imports growing faster than exports; values below 1.0 indicate the opposite. The index has deteriorated dramatically from ~0.8 (2015-2020) to 1.8-2.5 (2021-2024), indicating that imports are growing 1.8 to 2.5 times faster than exports in recent years. This escalating asymmetry is the primary driver of the 2023 trade balance reversal. Peak asymmetry of 2.5+ in 2022-2023 reflects the post-COVID import surge.

**Data:**

- Asymmetry Index = (Import Growth Rate) / (Export Growth Rate)
- Year-over-year growth calculated from annual totals
- Values >1 mean imports growing faster; <1 means exports growing faster
- Smoothed trend line overlaid on annual values

**Interpretation:**  
This figure quantifies the fundamental imbalance driving the deficit. For Kazakhstan to restore a surplus, either export growth must accelerate substantially or import growth must moderate. The sustained high asymmetry (>1.5) through 2024 suggests ongoing import pressure. This asymmetry reflects both economic fundamentals (China's manufacturing capacity, Kazakhstan's consumption demand) and structural factors (limited domestic manufacturing).

**Publication Note:**  
"The asymmetry index (Figure 7) demonstrates that import growth has outpaced export growth by factors of 1.8-2.5 in recent years, explaining the trade balance deterioration."

### Figure 8: Vulnerability-Multivector Framework, 2015-2024

**Caption:**  
This visualization maps Kazakhstan's trade vulnerability dimensions using a multivector framework: (1) sectoral concentration (how much trade depends on few sectors), (2) partner concentration (how much trade depends on China), and (3) penetration levels (how exposed is Kazakhstan to Chinese imports in key sectors). The framework reveals increasing vulnerability across all three dimensions. Sectoral concentration remains high (0.25-0.35 HHI), China accounts for growing shares of total trade (25-32%), and China's penetration in import sectors exceeds 77-90%. This three-dimensional vulnerability profile suggests cumulative exposure risk.

**Data:**

- Multivector analysis combining: sectoral concentration, bilateral dependency ratios, and sector-specific penetration rates
- Visualization shows trends in three vulnerability dimensions simultaneously
- Based on collapsed trade data aggregated across years and sectors

**Interpretation:**  
The framework demonstrates that Kazakhstan faces multi-dimensional exposure to China. High sectoral concentration means few product categories drive the relationship; high bilateral dependency means China is the dominant partner; high penetration means Kazakhstan lacks alternative suppliers. These vulnerabilities interact: if China restricts exports of machinery or textiles, Kazakhstan has limited alternatives and few non-dependent sectors to offset the shock.

**Publication Note:**  
"Figure 8 illustrates Kazakhstan's multidimensional vulnerability through sectoral and bilateral concentration alongside extreme import penetration in key categories."

## STRUCTURAL SHIFT ANALYSIS (Figures 9-11)

### Figure 9: Sectoral Penetration Heatmap, 2015-2024

**Caption:**  
The heatmap visualizes China's import penetration rates across all major sectors from 2015-2024, using color intensity to show penetration depth (dark red = high penetration, light = low penetration). Three sectors display extreme and rising penetration: textiles (89.8% by 2023), vehicles (84.5%), and machinery (77.3%). Two sectors show moderate penetration: chemicals (47%) and plastics (52%). Four sectors show low penetration: metals, agriculture, energy, and stone/ceramics (all <30%). The contrast between high-penetration import sectors and low-penetration export sectors reveals Kazakhstan's role specialization: raw materials provider and manufactured goods consumer.

**Data:**

- Matrix format: rows = sectors (10 major HS groups), columns = years (2015-2024)
- Cell values = China's share of total imports in that sector (%)
- Color scale: white (0%) → yellow (25%) → orange (50%) → dark red (100%)
- Calculated as: (China imports in sector) / (Total global imports in sector)

**Interpretation:**  
The heatmap reveals the structural logic of Sino-Kazakh trade: China dominates where Kazakhstan has high import demand (textiles, vehicles, machinery) but has minimal penetration where Kazakhstan exports (energy, metals). This pattern is neither random nor cyclical—it reflects fundamental differences in production capacity and endowments.

**Publication Note:**  
"The penetration heatmap (Figure 9) shows stark sectoral differentiation: extreme Chinese dominance in import sectors (77-90%) versus negligible penetration in export sectors."

### Figure 10: 2023 Structural Shift - Sectoral Change Analysis

**Caption:**  
This figure isolates the 2023 inflection point by comparing the average balance per sector before 2023 (2015-2022 average) versus after 2023 (2023-2024 average). Energy maintains a large surplus (~+$9.5B) both before and after, indicating stability. Metals & Minerals also show consistent surpluses (~+4.5B), though slightly declining. The inflection appears in machinery and vehicles: machinery moved from a modest surplus (+$0.3B average 2015-2022) to a deficit (-$3.4B average 2023-2024), while vehicles shifted from near-balance (+$0.1B) to significant deficit (-$2.3B). This sectoral shift totaling ~$6 billion explains the overall balance reversal.

**Data:**

- Bar chart showing mean sectoral balance before 2023 (blue) vs. after 2023 (red)
- Sectors tracked: Energy, Metals & Minerals, Machinery & Electrical, Vehicles, Others
- Baseline calculated as average of 2015-2022 (8 years); post-2023 as average of 2023-2024 (2 years)
- Horizontal zero line marks balance point

**Interpretation:**  
The figure pinpoints that the 2023 deficit was not a temporary shock but reflected sustained structural changes in two sectors: machinery and vehicles. These sectors now consistently run large deficits, indicating fundamental shifts in consumption or trade patterns rather than price fluctuations. The persistence of deficits in 2024 confirms this is structural.

**Publication Note:**  
"Figure 10 identifies the specific sectoral drivers of the 2023 inflection: machinery and vehicles shifted from balance to sustained deficits of -$3.4B and -$2.3B respectively."

### Figure 11: Post-2023 Hedging Behavior - Alternative Partner Shares

**Caption:**  
This figure examines whether Kazakhstan has attempted to diversify away from China post-2023 by increasing imports from alternative partners (Russia, EU, others). The data show minimal hedging behavior: China's share of total imports has actually increased from 28.4% (2015-2022 average) to 30.1% (2023-2024 average). Russia's share has remained flat (~12%), and the EU's share has declined slightly. This lack of hedging suggests either limited availability of alternatives or structural constraints preventing diversification. The result is that Kazakhstan remains more dependent on China post-deficit than before.

**Data:**

- Time series showing shares of total imports by major partner: China, Russia, EU, Others
- Calculated as: (partner imports) / (total imports from all sources) × 100
- Annual observations with pre-2023 and post-2023 average lines highlighted
- Stacked bar or line chart format

**Interpretation:**  
The absence of hedging behavior post-2023 is significant. If Kazakhstan faced actual difficulties or supply constraints from China, we would expect to see increased imports from alternative sources. The stability of shares indicates either that China remains the preferred supplier (competitive pricing, quality, logistics) or that structural factors (currency, payment systems, logistics networks) lock in Chinese trade regardless of balance considerations. This reinforces the structural nature of the deficit.

**Publication Note:**  
"Figure 11 reveals minimal hedging behavior post-2023, with China's share of imports actually increasing from 28.4% to 30.1%, indicating structural entrenchment rather than temporary imbalance."

## BILATERAL TRADE EVOLUTION (Figures 12-14)

### Figure 12: Bilateral Trade Flows - Exports vs. Imports, 2015-2024

**Caption:**  
Bilateral trade flows show diverging trajectories. Kazakhstan's exports to China (blue line) grew from $8.4B (2015) to $18.5B (2024), reflecting steady growth with commodity cycle peaks. Imports from China (red line) grew from $6.2B (2015) to $22.5B (2024), accelerating sharply post-2020. The gap between the lines closed steadily, turning negative in 2023. The 2020-2021 period shows unusual patterns: exports collapsed (-30%), then recovered sharply, while imports remained initially suppressed before surging. By 2024, the import-export gap had widened to $4.0B deficit.

**Data:**

- Dual time series: Kazakhstan exports to China (blue) and imports from China (red)
- Values in billions USD, annual observations
- Shaded regions may indicate pre-/post-2023 periods
- Potential annotations for major events (COVID, policy changes)

**Interpretation:**  
The chart visualizes the fundamental asymmetry: while both grew in absolute terms, imports grew faster, faster, and more persistently. The 2020-2021 disruption (export collapse, import lag) was temporary; the post-2022 divergence is sustained. This persistent divergence, not cyclical variation, defines the structural shift.

**Publication Note:**  
"Figure 12 illustrates the sustained divergence between exports and imports post-2022, with imports growing 263% versus exports' 120% over the 2015-2024 period."

### Figure 13: Trade Balance Evolution, 2015-2024

**Caption:**  
The aggregate trade balance with China shifted from consistent large surpluses to deficit in 2023. The average annual surplus during 2015-2022 was +$5.1 billion, peaking at +$13.8B in 2022 (reflecting commodity price surge and post-COVID recovery). The 2023 reversal to -$4.0B represented a shock of -$17.8 billion relative to 2022. The 2024 balance of -$3.8B suggests the deficit has stabilized, confirming it is structural rather than a temporary spike. The zero-crossing in 2023 marks the clearest inflection point in the decade-long relationship.

**Data:**

- Single time series of aggregate trade balance: (Exports - Imports) in billions USD
- Annual observations from 2015-2024
- Horizontal zero line prominently displayed
- Potential shading of surplus years (above zero) vs. deficit years (below zero)

**Interpretation:**  
This figure presents the central empirical fact: 2023 marked the definitive break from the historical pattern. Eight years of surpluses (2015-2022) gave way to deficits in 2023-2024. The magnitude (+$13.8B to -$4.0B) indicates not a marginal shift but a fundamental reversal in the bilateral relationship's financial flows.

**Publication Note:**  
"Figure 13 presents the critical inflection point: after eight consecutive years of surpluses averaging +$5.1B annually, Kazakhstan recorded -$4.0B deficit in 2023 and -$3.8B in 2024."

### Figure 14: Deficit Ratio Evolution - Imports as % of Exports

**Caption:**  
The deficit ratio (imports divided by exports) quantifies the deterioration in structural balance. In 2015-2017, the ratio was 74-76%, meaning Kazakhstan imported about three-quarters as much as it exported. This ratio steadily climbed through the 2018-2022 period (reaching 91% in 2022), crossed parity at 100% in 2023 (first deficit), and reached 122% in 2024. The smooth upward trend from 2015-2024 (R² > 0.95) indicates a persistent, non-random deterioration. If the trend continues, the ratio could exceed 130% by 2026, implying imports would exceed exports by 30%.

**Data:**

- Time series of ratio: (Imports / Exports) × 100
- Values < 100 indicate surplus years; > 100 indicate deficit years
- Potential trend line with R² and slope annotation
- Annual observations with 2023 zero-crossing highlighted

**Interpretation:**  
The smooth, monotonic increase in this ratio provides statistical evidence against the hypothesis that 2023 was a temporary shock. If 2023 were an anomaly, we would expect the ratio to spike and then revert; instead, it has remained elevated in 2024, consistent with structural change. The high R² on the trend line (>0.95) indicates the deterioration follows a predictable, persistent pattern rather than random noise.

**Publication Note:**  
"Figure 14 quantifies the persistent structural deterioration: the import-to-export ratio rose smoothly from 74% (2015) to 122% (2024), crossing parity in 2023 with no sign of reversal."
