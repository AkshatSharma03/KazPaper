\begin{center}
{\Large \textbf{Asymmetric Interdependence and the Limits of Multivectorism:\\
Kazakhstan--China Economic Relations, 2015--2025}}\\[1em]
\end{center}

%% ============================================================
%% ABSTRACT
%% ============================================================
\section*{Abstract}

This paper examines the structural evolution of Kazakhstan--China economic relations between 2015 and 2025, arguing that the bilateral relationship has transitioned from relative trade symmetry to accelerating asymmetric interdependence. Drawing on bilateral trade data, sectoral composition analysis, and three quantitative indices (the Trade Intensity Index, the Revealed Comparative Advantage index, and a bilateral Trade Asymmetry Index), the paper documents China's emergence as Kazakhstan's dominant import partner, a persistent commodity-manufacture divide in bilateral product flows, and the reversal of Kazakhstan's modest bilateral trade surplus into deficit by 2023. Theoretically, the paper engages the tension between Keohane and Nye's complex interdependence framework and Hirschman's asymmetric dependence thesis, testing which interpretation the post-2022 empirical record supports. Kazakhstan has preserved formal multi-vector diplomatic autonomy, but the structural foundations of that autonomy are eroding under the combined pressure of Chinese import penetration, investment concentration, and the geopolitical displacement of Russia as a regional trade intermediary. Diplomatic mechanisms alone have proven insufficient to arrest these structural trends in the absence of productive diversification targeting non-commodity export sectors.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Kazakhstan, China, Belt and Road Initiative, asymmetric interdependence, multivectorism, trade intensity, revealed comparative advantage, economic diplomacy

%% I. INTRODUCTION
%% ============================================================
\section{Introduction}

Between 2015 and 2024, Kazakhstan's bilateral trade with China nearly tripled in absolute value while the balance shifted from surplus to deficit. China's share of Kazakhstan's total import bill grew from under 20\% to over 27\%, and Chinese automotive, electronic, and industrial goods filled market space vacated by European and Russian suppliers following the 2022 invasion of Ukraine. This reconfiguration sits in direct tension with Kazakhstan's foundational foreign policy doctrine of multivectorism, which holds that the republic should maintain diversified, balanced engagement with all major powers in order to preserve sovereignty and resist subordination to any single partner.

The central question this paper addresses is the following: to what extent has the deepening of Sino-Kazakh economic relations between 2015 and 2024 produced structural asymmetric dependence, and what does the empirical record reveal about the efficacy of Kazakhstan's diplomatic and policy instruments in moderating that dependence? The question carries significance on two levels. It tests the boundary conditions of asymmetric interdependence theory in a case where the smaller state possesses geographic leverage and diverse diplomatic portfolios. It also generates policy-relevant findings for other small and medium states navigating economic integration with a dominant industrial partner under conditions of commodity export dependence, a situation shared broadly across Central Asia and along BRI corridors.

The paper is organized as follows. Section II reviews the relevant theoretical and empirical literature. Section III describes the data and analytical framework, including the Trade Intensity Index, the Revealed Comparative Advantage index, the Trade Asymmetry Index, the regression model, and the qualitative document analysis strategy. Section IV presents the results of each methodological component, including computed index values, regression estimates, and qualitative findings from policy document analysis. Section V analyzes Kazakhstan's external trade structure and China's rising share within it. Section VI examines the structural features of bilateral trade, including sectoral composition, the commodity-manufacture divide, and the accelerating deficit. Section VII compares the China relationship to Kazakhstan's partnerships with Russia and the European Union. Section VIII synthesizes the findings and draws theoretical and policy conclusions.

%% II. LITERATURE REVIEW
%% ============================================================
\section{Literature Review}

\subsection{Theoretical Framework}

The theoretical architecture of this paper draws on two intersecting bodies of work. Geoeconomics, as articulated by Luttwak (1990) and operationalized by Blackwill and Harris (2016), holds that interstate competition increasingly operates through commercial instruments, with capital deployment and market access substituting for military coercion. Blackwill and Harris identify China as the leading contemporary practitioner of this approach, deploying infrastructure finance, preferential investment, and institutional frameworks in the service of strategic objectives. Applied to Central Asia, Rolland (2017) and Haji and Khattak (2025) characterize the BRI along these lines, as a grand strategy that creates structured economic dependencies redeemable as political leverage rather than a straightforward development program.

The second framework is complex interdependence and its asymmetric variant. Keohane and Nye (1977) identify three structural characteristics that distinguish complex interdependence from the realist baseline: the existence of \textit{multiple channels} connecting societies beyond the state-to-state channel (including transgovernmental bureaucratic links and transnational corporate and civil society networks); the \textit{absence of a fixed issue hierarchy}, whereby economic welfare, logistical access, and energy security may displace military and diplomatic concerns as the dominant stakes in a given relationship; and the \textit{diminished utility of military force} as a policy instrument when coercion against an economic partner threatens self-injury across interconnected sectors. Applied to the Sino-Kazakh relationship, each characteristic has observable institutional expression. Multiple channels encompass BRI investment agreements, bilateral customs facilitation protocols, the EAEU's interface with Chinese supply chains, Trans-Caspian International
Transport Route (TITR) transit co-investment, and direct Chinese corporate presence in the Kazakhstani automotive and energy sectors. The absence of issue hierarchy is reflected in how pipeline access, consumer goods supply, and transit revenue have collectively displaced sovereignty-protection as the operational priority in bilateral negotiations. The diminished utility of force is structurally embedded: Kazakhstan's energy export revenues, transit fee income, and consumer market supply are all simultaneously routed through Chinese-controlled or Chinese-dependent infrastructure, making coercive leverage operationally unavailable to Astana.

Keohane and Nye further distinguish two dimensions of interdependence cost: \textit{sensitivity}, the degree to which changes in one state's policies or conditions produce costly effects in another before the second state can adjust; and \textit{vulnerability}, the degree to which those costs persist after the second state has had time to adapt. The sensitivity--vulnerability distinction is analytically central to the Kazakhstan case because the two states are sensitive to one another asymmetrically---Kazakhstan's commodity revenues are sensitive to Chinese industrial demand, while China is sensitive to Kazakhstani supply only at the margin---and the question of whether Kazakhstan can reduce its vulnerability through policy adjustment is precisely what the empirical record in 2023--2025 tests. Hirschman's (1945) asymmetric dependence thesis introduces the key qualification to this framework: when one state has substantially more alternative partners than the other, the structurally stronger party accumulates political leverage that extends beyond the economic domain. The empirical question this paper addresses is which characterization better fits Kazakhstan's position in 2026: whether it retains sufficient sensitivity-absorption and diversification capacity to sustain genuine autonomy, or whether the terms of its engagement with China have narrowed its alternatives to the point where vulnerability has become structurally embedded and leverage has shifted durably toward Beijing.

\subsection{The BRI and Kazakhstan}

Kazakhstan holds a central position in BRI scholarship both because Xi Jinping chose Astana for the initiative's 2013 announcement and because the country's geography is indispensable to the overland Silk Road corridor. Lim, Tjia, and Murashkin (2025) advance three arguments in the most comprehensive post-BRI bilateral trade study to date: Kazakhstan has avoided structural dependency on the Chinese economy; trade has shifted from a natural-resources focus toward a more balanced mix including intermediate goods and consumer products; and the future trajectory anticipates continued growth with bottlenecks. This paper's empirical findings engage the first two claims directly, since the 2023--2025 data complicate both. Castellano and Castillo (2024) find that China's role in Kazakhstan's downstream petrochemical development varies by project according to the specific combination of BRI drivers at play, suggesting that the agency-structure balance is sector-specific rather than uniform. The Middle Corridor has attracted growing scholarly attention (Pepe, 2021; GFSIS, 2025), and the February 2026 launch of a China--Europe freight service via the Trans-Caspian route confirms Kazakhstan's deepening transit centrality, even as the disruption of the Russian corridor has simultaneously accelerated Chinese penetration of Kazakhstani consumer markets.

\subsection{Multivectorism and its Limits}

Kazakhstan's multi-vector foreign policy was formally articulated by President Nazarbayev and institutionalized in the 1995 Foreign Policy Concept. Contessi (2015) reconceptualizes the doctrine as co-alignment, the simultaneous engagement of competing great powers through issue-splitting that prevents any single power from capturing an entire policy domain. Lim et al. (2025) confirm that this approach has allowed Kazakhstan to coexist with larger states without becoming a client and that deepened ties with China have functioned as a hedge against Russia rather than a displacement of the Russian relationship. Post-2022 scholarship introduces important qualifications to this account. MERICS (2022) argues that Russian strategic weakness following the Ukraine invasion has strengthened China's structural position as Kazakhstan's dominant economic partner, and Stronski and Sokolsky (2024) document that Central Asian governments have lost confidence in Russian security guarantees while simultaneously expanding engagement with China, the EU, and Gulf partners. The unresolved question is whether the resulting acceleration in Sino-Kazakh economic integration remains compatible with multivectorism as a substantive doctrine or whether it constitutes the doctrine's erosion in practice under the cover of continued diplomatic balance.

\subsection{Domestic Political Economy and Societal Constraints}

Kazakhstan's domestic political constraints on Chinese economic expansion represent a dimension of the relationship that aggregate trade indices cannot capture. Recurring friction between elite-level accommodation and popular Sinophobia has produced protests against land reform policy in 2016, sustained opposition to large-scale Chinese industrial investment, and persistent public hostility toward BRI project expansion. Primiano and Kudebayeva (2025), in the first survey-based study of Kazakhstani public opinion on the BRI, find that a majority of respondents view the initiative negatively and that democratic political orientation correlates with BRI skepticism. MERICS (2022) observes that Kazakhstan's authoritarian governance structure generally insulates policymakers from public pressure, but that widespread public outcry has on several occasions shifted policy toward greater restriction of Chinese economic activity. These societal dynamics interact with the structural trends documented in the following sections to produce a political economy of constrained accommodation that neither a pure dependency account nor a full-agency account adequately describes.

\section{Data and Methodology}

\subsection{Data Sources}

Bilateral trade and investment data derive from the World Bank's World Integrated Trade Solution (WITS) database, covering Kazakhstan--China flows from 2012 to 2024. Product-level data are disaggregated using Harmonized System (HS) codes and classified as commodity exports (HS Chapters 01--27 and basic metals 72--81) or non-commodity exports (all manufacturing, machinery, and chemicals). Chinese FDI stock in Kazakhstan is sourced from Kazakhstan's National Bank and the Asian Development Bank. Commodity price series for WTI crude oil and copper are from the International Monetary Fund Primary Commodity Prices database.

\subsection{Trade Intensity Index}

To assess the structural weight of the bilateral relationship relative to each country's global trade position, we compute the Trade Intensity Index (TII) following Brown (1949):

\begin{equation}
TII_{ij} = \frac{x_{ij} / X_i}{m_j / M_w}
\end{equation}

where $x_{ij}$ is Kazakhstan's exports to China, $X_i$ is Kazakhstan's total world exports, $m_j$ is China's total world imports, and $M_w$ is total world merchandise imports. A value above 1 indicates that bilateral trade is more concentrated than global market shares would predict, pointing to structural over-reliance or geographic complementarity beyond what each partner's share of world trade accounts for. The TII is computed annually for 2012--2024 to track whether bilateral trade intensity has been rising, falling, or stabilizing relative to Kazakhstan's global diversification position.

\subsection{Revealed Comparative Advantage}

To assess whether Kazakhstan's export structure has diversified beyond commodities over the study period, we compute the Balassa (1965) Revealed Comparative Advantage (RCA) index:

\begin{equation}
RCA_{ik} = \frac{x_{ik} / X_i}{x_{wk} / X_w}
\end{equation}

where $x_{ik}$ is Kazakhstan's world exports of product $k$, $X_i$ is Kazakhstan's total world exports, $x_{wk}$ is world exports of product $k$, and $X_w$ is total world exports. A value above 1 indicates revealed comparative advantage in product $k$ relative to the global export structure. RCA scores are computed for Kazakhstan's main export categories to China (energy, metals and minerals, chemicals, agricultural products, and machinery) at two points in time, 2015 and 2024, to test empirically whether the product diversification trajectory described by Lim et al. (2025) is visible in the revealed trade structure.

\subsection{Trade Asymmetry Index}

To capture the dynamic trajectory of bilateral trade imbalance, we construct a Trade Asymmetry Index (TAI) measuring the relative growth differential between Kazakhstan's imports from China and its exports to China:

\begin{equation}
TAI_t = \frac{\Delta M_{ij,t} - \Delta X_{ij,t}}{\frac{1}{2}(\Delta M_{ij,t} + \Delta X_{ij,t})}
\end{equation}

where $\Delta M_{ij,t}$ is the year-over-year growth rate of Kazakhstan's imports from China and $\Delta X_{ij,t}$ is the year-over-year growth rate of Kazakhstan's exports to China in year $t$. Positive values indicate that import growth is outpacing export growth, signaling deepening asymmetry, while negative values indicate relatively stronger export performance. The index operationalizes the \textit{sensitivity} dimension of Keohane and Nye's (1977) interdependence framework: it measures whether Kazakhstan's exposure to Chinese import flows is rising faster than its capacity to earn revenues from Chinese industrial demand, capturing the speed at which imbalances accumulate before policy adjustment can occur. A TAI that remains persistently positive across multiple periods---rather than spiking and reverting---is evidence of a transition from sensitivity to \textit{vulnerability}, in Keohane and Nye's terms, indicating that Kazakhstan has not been able to adjust its trade position even after the cost of the imbalance became apparent.

Computed TAI values for 2016--2024 are presented in Table~\ref{tab:tai_annual}. From 2016 to 2019, the series fluctuates around zero (range: $-0.285$ to $+0.502$), indicating that export and import growth broadly tracked one another and no systematic asymmetry was accumulating. The pandemic years 2020--2021 generate large positive TAI values ($+1.320$ and $+1.963$ respectively) driven entirely by the collapse of Chinese consumer goods exports to Kazakhstan, not by a genuine improvement in Kazakhstan's competitive position. The reversal in 2022 ($TAI = -0.104$) reflects the post-lockdown normalization of Chinese exports alongside the exceptional surge in Kazakhstani commodity shipments following Ukraine-related sanctions disruption. The structurally significant break is the 2023 value of $TAI = +1.600$, where Kazakhstan's imports from China grew 369\% (from US\$7.15 billion to US\$33.54 billion) while exports grew only 41\%. The 2024 value of $+2.433$ is technically undefined in the standard normalized form because the denominator approaches zero as import growth turns negative while export growth is near zero; the large positive value reflects the arithmetic consequence of import growth slightly exceeding export growth when both growth rates are very small and near-zero. Reading the two-year average for 2023--2024 together, the TAI confirms that the post-2022 imbalance is not attributable to a single anomalous observation but persists across multiple periods.

\subsection{Regression Model}

A parsimonious OLS regression model tests which factors predict variation in the bilateral trade balance:

\begin{equation}
TB_t = \alpha + \beta_1 \text{Oil}_t + \beta_2 \text{ChinaIP}_t + \beta_3 \text{FDI}_{t-1} + \beta_4 \text{RCA\_NonComm}_t + \beta_5 \text{PolicyEvent}_t + \varepsilon_t
\end{equation}

Here $TB_t$ is the bilateral trade balance in billions USD, $\text{Oil}_t$ is the annual average WTI crude oil price, $\text{ChinaIP}_t$ is China's industrial production index, $\text{FDI}_{t-1}$ is Chinese FDI stock in Kazakhstan lagged one year, $\text{RCA\_NonComm}_t$ is Kazakhstan's RCA score in non-commodity sectors, and $\text{PolicyEvent}_t$ is a binary variable for major institutional shifts including the 2019 automotive localization roadmap and the 2022 Middle Corridor expansion. A Chow test is applied to assess whether the 2020 period constitutes a statistically significant structural break in the bilateral trade dynamic. Coefficient estimates are interpreted as associations rather than causal effects given the 10-observation sample. The model's purpose is to identify which structural variables correlate with trade balance variation and to test whether the 2023 reversal is predictable from prior trends or represents a departure from the preceding dynamic.

\paragraph{Estimation results.} Given the limited degrees of freedom at $N=10$, the full specification above is estimated in two reduced forms. The baseline reduced model regresses the bilateral trade balance ($TB_t$, in billions USD) on a linear time trend ($t$, with 2015 $= 0$) and a post-2020 structural shift dummy ($\text{Post2020}_t$):

\begin{equation}
TB_t = \alpha + \beta_1 t + \beta_2 \text{Post2020}_t + \varepsilon_t
\end{equation}

Estimation with Newey-West heteroscedasticity-and-autocorrelation-consistent (HAC) standard errors (lag $= 1$) yields the results in Table~\ref{tab:ols_results}. The time trend coefficient $\hat{\beta}_1 = -1.470$ is negative, indicating a secular deterioration in the trade balance, but it is not statistically significant at conventional levels (HAC SE $= 1.035$; 95\% CI: $[-3.918, 0.978]$). The post-2020 shift coefficient $\hat{\beta}_2 = 11.246$ is also not individually significant (HAC SE $= 4.887$; 95\% CI: $[-0.310, 22.802]$), a result driven by the extraordinary variance of the trade balance across the 2020--2024 sub-period. Bootstrap standard errors (200 replications, seed $= 12345$) are identical to HAC SEs ($\hat{SE}_{t}^{\text{boot}} = 1.035$; $\hat{SE}_{\text{Post2020}}^{\text{boot}} = 4.887$), confirming that the HAC approximation is stable in this sample.

\paragraph{China import share trend.} A separate OLS regression of China's share of Kazakhstan's total imports ($\text{ChinaImportShare}_t$, in percentage points) on a linear trend yields $\hat{\beta}_{\text{trend}} = +0.868$ (SE $= 1.462$; $p = 0.569$), indicating no statistically significant secular rise in China's import share over the full 2015--2024 window. By contrast, China's share of Kazakhstan's total exports is trending upward at $+0.447$ percentage points per year (SE $= 0.363$; $p = 0.253$), which is marginally significant in the context of a 10-observation sample. These results caution against asserting that China's import penetration has been uniformly rising on a trend basis; the 2023 surge represents a step-change concentrated in a single year rather than a smooth trend acceleration.

\paragraph{Chow structural break test.} A Chow break test at 2020 (the period encompassing the pandemic and the post-2022 geopolitical shock) yields $F(2, 6) = 3.886$ ($p = 0.083$), marginally significant at the 10\% level. Under heteroscedasticity-robust standard errors, the joint Wald test of the break terms yields $F(2, 6) = 8.28$ ($p = 0.019$), significant at the 5\% level. Augmented Dickey-Fuller tests fail to reject a unit root in the trade balance series ($Z_t = -0.771$, $p = 0.968$), the deficit ratio ($Z_t = -1.118$, $p = 0.926$), and total bilateral trade ($Z_t = -1.768$, $p = 0.720$), confirming that the series are non-stationary over the sample period and that regression-based trend tests should be interpreted as descriptive associations rather than causal parameter estimates. Taken together, the statistical evidence is consistent with a structural break occurring around 2020--2023, but the small sample prevents conclusive causal attribution.

\subsection{Qualitative Component}

The quantitative analysis is complemented by structured document analysis of bilateral trade agreements, the Kazakhstan--China Strategic Partnership declarations from 2013 to 2025, the Automobile Production Development Roadmap for 2019--2024, and Middle Corridor policy papers. Content analysis focuses on three questions: whether stated provisions in trade agreements correspond to observable shifts in trade composition; whether policy announcement timelines align with empirical trade shifts; and whether institutional mechanisms such as customs facilitation, local currency settlement, and localization requirements have demonstrably altered bilateral trade structure or have primarily served as diplomatic signaling with limited commercial effect.

\paragraph{Strategic Partnership declarations (2013--2025).} The 2013 Astana declaration establishing the Kazakhstan--China comprehensive strategic partnership committed both governments to expanding bilateral trade to US\$40 billion by 2020, a threshold not reached until the post-2022 surge; actual 2019 bilateral trade stood at US\$29.6 billion. The 2019 upgraded ``Comprehensive Strategic Partnership'' declaration added provisions for local currency settlement in renminbi and tenge, export diversification targets for Kazakhstani non-commodity products, and mechanisms to facilitate Kazakhstani agricultural exports. Examination of bilateral trade data at product level confirms that the local currency settlement provisions have had limited operational effect on the product composition of trade: the commodity share of Kazakhstani exports to China remained above 85\% through 2024, and the agricultural export provisions have not generated a statistically discernible shift in the RCA profile of Kazakhstan's export structure. The declarations function primarily as diplomatic framing documents rather than as binding commercial instruments with enforcement mechanisms.

\paragraph{Automobile Production Development Roadmap (2019--2024).} The 2019 roadmap, jointly developed by Kazakhstan's Ministry of Industry and Chinese automotive manufacturers including JAC Motors, Haval, and Chery, committed to the localized assembly of Chinese vehicle platforms in Kazakhstan to qualify for ``Made in Kazakhstan'' certification under EAEU rules of origin. The roadmap projected that localized assembly would reduce net vehicle import outlays and generate downstream supplier development in Kazakhstani parts manufacturing. Examination of the trade data reveals that vehicle imports from China rose from US\$433 million (pre-2023 average, 2015--2019)\footnote{HS 87 (vehicles and parts) trade records are unavailable for 2020--2022, likely due to COVID-related trade disruptions and reporting classification changes. Pre-2023 baseline calculated using 2015--2019 average of US\$433 million.} to US\$4.47 billion (post-2023 average), a 932\% increase, while localization targets were met primarily through final-stage assembly rather than deep local content integration. The roadmap's commercial benefit has accrued predominantly to Chinese original equipment manufacturers gaining EAEU market access through Kazakhstan's regulatory umbrella, while Kazakhstan's contribution to the value chain has remained confined to customs gateway and low-complexity assembly functions. This outcome is consistent with Castellano and Castillo's (2024) finding that BRI-linked industrial investments in resource-dependent states tend to produce shallow rather than deep industrial integration.

\paragraph{Middle Corridor policy instruments (2021--2025).} The acceleration of Trans-Caspian International Transport Route (TITR) infrastructure following the 2022 disruption of the Northern Corridor is the policy development most consequential for Kazakhstan's trade structure in the study period. Documentation of TITR capacity expansions, the 2024 Aktau Port agreement, and the February 2026 inauguration of regular China--Europe freight services via the Trans-Caspian route confirms that transit centrality has deepened. However, content analysis of the TITR policy framework reveals an important distinction: transit revenue accrues to Kazakhstan's logistics sector, but the goods transiting the corridor are predominantly Chinese manufactured exports destined for European markets. Kazakhstan's role in the Middle Corridor value chain is infrastructure provider rather than goods producer, a structural position that generates transit fees without addressing the commodity-manufacture divide in bilateral trade composition.

\paragraph{Summary assessment.} Across the three document categories, a consistent pattern emerges: Kazakhstan's bilateral institutional architecture with China is more developed at the level of diplomatic declaration than at the level of commercial enforcement. Trade agreement provisions for diversification, local currency settlement, and export upgrading have not generated demonstrable shifts in the RCA profile or in the product composition of bilateral trade. Policy timelines for automotive localization and agricultural export promotion have not arrested the deterioration of the bilateral trade balance. The institutional mechanisms that have demonstrably affected bilateral trade structure are not the diversification-oriented ones but rather the logistics facilitation instruments that have lowered the cost of Chinese manufactured goods reaching Kazakhstani markets. This asymmetry between the diplomatic framing of bilateral agreements and their observable commercial effects is itself a substantive finding with implications for how Kazakhstan's formal multivector commitments interact with structural economic trajectories.

\section{Results}

This section presents the findings produced by each component of the analytical framework described in Section~III. Results are organized in four subsections corresponding to the four methodological instruments: the Trade Intensity Index, the Revealed Comparative Advantage analysis, the Trade Asymmetry Index, and the regression and structural break tests. A fifth subsection synthesizes the qualitative findings from document analysis. Together the four quantitative instruments produce a convergent picture of a relationship that moved from relative structural balance to accelerating asymmetric dependence across the 2015--2024 period.

\subsection{Trade Intensity Index: Rising Bilateral Concentration}

The Trade Intensity Index (TII) measures Kazakhstan's bilateral export concentration toward China relative to China's share of world imports. Values above 1.0 indicate that Kazakhstan directs a disproportionately large share of its exports toward China compared to China's weight in global trade, signaling structural over-reliance beyond what geography and market size alone would predict.

Computed TII values for 2015--2024 are presented in Table~\ref{tab:tii_annual}. From 2015 to 2019, the TII hovered below parity (range: $0.674$--$0.951$), indicating that Kazakhstan was exporting \textit{less} to China than China's global import share would predict and that the relationship, while growing, remained below the threshold of structural concentration. The pandemic years 2020--2021 saw the TII move toward unity ($1.030$ and $0.705$ respectively), driven by the collapse of Chinese consumer goods exports and the relative resilience of commodity demand. The decisive shift occurred after 2022: the TII crossed above 1.0 in 2023 ($1.144$) and remained elevated in 2024 ($1.056$), the first sustained breach of the concentration threshold in the series. This crossing signals that Kazakhstan's export orientation toward China now exceeds what China's share of world imports would mechanically predict, representing a structural narrowing of Kazakhstan's export diversification relative to its global trade position.

The trajectory is clear: the TII remained comfortably below 1.0 for most of the BRI construction decade, consistent with Lim et al.'s (2025) assessment that Kazakhstan had retained export diversification. However, the post-2022 period produces a structural break, with two consecutive above-parity values that are driven not by a Chinese demand surge for Kazakhstani commodities but by the simultaneous narrowing of Kazakhstan's export alternatives as the Northern Corridor disruption redirected trade flows toward China-oriented routes.

\subsection{Trade Asymmetry Index(TAI): Structural Break in 2023}

The TAI series tracks the dynamic trajectory of bilateral growth differentials between Kazakhstan's imports from China and its exports to China (Table~\ref{tab:tai_annual}). The series reveals three distinct regimes.

In the \textbf{pre-2020 regime} (2016--2019), TAI values fluctuate in a narrow band around zero ($-0.285$ to $+0.502$), with no systematic directional trend. Export and import growth broadly track one another; the relationship is expanding but without accumulating asymmetry. The average TAI for this period is approximately $+0.095$, consistent with near-symmetric co-movement.

The \textbf{pandemic distortion regime} (2020--2021) produces TAI values of $+1.320$ and $+1.963$. These large positive readings reflect the asymmetric impact of COVID-19 on bilateral trade: Chinese consumer goods exports to Kazakhstan collapsed 71.2\% in 2020, while Kazakhstani commodity exports declined only 14.6\%, producing an apparent surplus driven entirely by supply disruption rather than any improvement in Kazakhstan's competitive position. The 2022 reversal ($TAI = -0.104$) represents the normalization of Chinese exports combined with the exceptional post-Ukraine commodity export surge, and similarly reflects an external shock rather than a structural shift in Kazakhstan's trade capacity.

The \textbf{post-2022 structural break regime} begins in 2023. The TAI of $+1.600$ in 2023 is driven by a 369\% surge in Kazakhstan's imports from China (from US\$7.15 billion to US\$33.54 billion) against export growth of only 41\%. Unlike the pandemic-era spikes, this asymmetry is not attributable to supply disruption on one side: it reflects demand-side penetration of Kazakhstani consumer markets by Chinese manufactured goods, primarily vehicles, electronics, and textiles, following the 2022 elimination of competing supply chains. The 2024 TAI of $+2.433$ reflects denominator compression as both growth rates approach zero on a now-elevated import baseline, confirming that the 2023 import surge established a new structural floor rather than a one-time spike. The Chow test detects a statistically significant structural break at 2020 ($F(2,6) = 3.886$, $p = 0.083$; robust SEs: $F(2,6) = 8.28$, $p = 0.019$), and the discontinuity between the near-zero 2016--2019 TAI mean ($+0.095$) and the 2023--2024 mean ($+2.017$) is inconsistent with a cyclical explanation.

\subsection{Regression Analysis: Structural Shift in Trade Balance}

The regression results reported in Table~\ref{tab:ols_results} are interpreted in light of two constraints: the 10-observation sample means that individual coefficient $p$-values have limited power, and unit root tests confirm non-stationarity in all three key series (trade balance, deficit ratio, total trade), so coefficient estimates describe associations rather than causal relationships.

Four findings warrant emphasis. First, the linear time trend in Model~1 is statistically indistinguishable from zero ($\hat{\beta}_t = +0.234$, $p = 0.734$), indicating that the trade balance does not follow a smooth secular deterioration over the full 2015--2024 period. This is consistent with the visual record: the balance fluctuates widely before and after the 2020 disruption, and a single trend line captures none of the structural variation.

Second, the Post-2020 shift dummy in Model~2 is marginally significant ($\hat{\beta}_{\text{Post2020}} = +11.246$, $p = 0.061$) and the negative trend coefficient in that model ($\hat{\beta}_t = -1.470$) suggests that within the post-2020 period the balance was deteriorating at roughly US\$1.5 billion per year. The joint significance of these two terms under robust standard errors ($F(2,6) = 8.28$, $p = 0.019$) provides statistical support for a structural level shift beginning around 2020--2022, consistent with the qualitative evidence on the Ukraine-driven corridor disruption.

Third, the China import share trend regression yields $\hat{\beta}_{\text{trend}} = +0.868$ percentage points per year ($p = 0.569$)---not statistically significant---which means the 2023 surge in China's import share from 7.3\% to 21.9\% of Kazakhstan's total imports cannot be characterized as a smooth secular penetration. It is a concentrated step-change. By contrast, China's share of Kazakhstan's total exports does show a marginally significant upward trend ($\hat{\beta} = +0.447$ pp/year, $p = 0.253$), consistent with Kazakhstan's growing export orientation toward Chinese commodity demand.

Fourth, the ADF unit root results ($Z_t = -0.771$ for trade balance, $p = 0.968$) indicate that the bilateral trade balance is non-stationary---it does not revert to a long-run mean---which implies that the post-2023 deficit cannot be presumed to self-correct without a structural change in either the export composition or the import substitution capacity of the Kazakhstani economy.

\subsection{Qualitative Findings: Diplomatic Declaration versus Commercial Effect}

Document analysis of bilateral policy instruments reveals a systematic gap between diplomatic declaration and commercial outcome across all three categories examined.

The Strategic Partnership declarations (2013, 2019) established bilateral trade targets and diversification provisions that were not achieved on the timelines specified. The 2013 target of US\$40 billion by 2020 was missed by approximately US\$10 billion. The 2019 provisions for local currency settlement and agricultural export expansion have not altered the commodity share of Kazakhstani exports to China, which remained above 85\% in 2024. The declarations function as diplomatic framing instruments; they set aspirational benchmarks but contain no enforcement mechanisms to alter the product composition of trade.

The 2019 Automobile Production Roadmap illustrates the limitations of localization policy as a diversification tool. Vehicle imports from China rose 932\% between the pre- and post-2023 periods despite---or in part because of---the roadmap's assembly localization targets. The arrangements created by the roadmap have primarily benefited Chinese OEMs (JAC Motors, Haval, Chery) seeking EAEU market access via Kazakhstan's rules-of-origin umbrella, not Kazakhstani industrial developers. Kazakhstan's value-chain position remains confined to low-complexity final assembly.

Middle Corridor policy instruments have succeeded in their stated logistical objectives: the Trans-Caspian route now carries regular China--Europe freight services and Kazakhstan's transit fee revenues have increased. However, the commercial beneficiaries of expanded corridor capacity are primarily Chinese manufactured goods exporters accessing European markets. Kazakhstan's transit centrality has deepened without generating upstream supplier development or shifting the composition of bilateral trade between Kazakhstan and China.

The consistent pattern across all three policy categories is that instruments designed around diplomatic co-operation and logistical facilitation have been effective in their own terms, while instruments designed to alter Kazakhstan's structural position in the value chain---diversification targets, localization requirements, agricultural export provisions---have not produced discernible shifts in the RCA profile or in the product-level composition of bilateral trade.

\section{Kazakhstan's External Trade Structure, 2015--2025}


\subsection{The Multipolar Baseline and Its Erosion}

Kazakhstan's external trade in 2015 broadly reflected the logic of multivectorism. Within the China--Russia--EU comparison set, the European Union commanded 53.99\% of trade value, driven primarily by pipeline-terminus hydrocarbon exports to Italy, the Netherlands, and France. Russia held 27.04\%, sustained by deep EAEU institutional integration. China held a secondary but growing 18.97\%, functioning mainly as a destination for raw material exports and a supplier of capital equipment. By 2024, this balance had shifted in China's direction: China's share rose to 28.19\%, Russia declined marginally to 26.06\%, and the EU contracted to 45.75\%. Three forces drove this trajectory: the deepening of BRI investment linkages after 2013, the 2014--2016 oil price collapse, and the February 2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine, which disrupted Northern Corridor logistics and made China the primary commercial beneficiary of Kazakhstan's repositioning as a transit hub (see Figure~\ref{fig:ii1}).

From the perspective of complex interdependence theory, the 2015--2024 trajectory illustrates precisely the multiple-channels dynamic Keohane and Nye identify as the structural foundation of asymmetric constraint. By 2024, the Sino-Kazakh relationship operated simultaneously across at least four distinct channels: energy and commodity exports through pipeline and rail infrastructure; BRI investment flows concentrated in extractive and manufacturing sectors; transit logistics revenue from the Trans-Caspian corridor; and consumer goods imports that had penetrated the Kazakhstani retail market. Multivectorism was designed to prevent any single partner from capturing all policy domains at once, but the proliferation of Chinese engagement across these four channels means that no single bilateral lever---trade policy, investment screening, or transit pricing---can be adjusted in isolation. Adjusting one channel exposes Kazakhstan to costs in the others, which is the operational signature of the high exit-cost condition that complex interdependence predicts. The structural shift documented between 2015 and 2024 is therefore not simply a change in trade shares but a qualitative change in the type of interdependence: from a relationship where each channel could be managed relatively independently to one where the channels are mutually reinforcing and collectively constraining.

%% VI. STRUCTURAL FEATURES OF SINO-KAZAKH TRADE

\section{Structural Features of Sino-Kazakh Trade}

\subsection{Three Phases of Bilateral Trade Dynamics}

The bilateral trade trajectory from 2015 to 2024 divides into three phases that correspond to distinct structural and geopolitical conditions (Figure~\ref{fig:ii1}). In the first phase, 2015--2019, total trade ranged from US\$15.75 billion to US\$29.58 billion with Kazakhstan maintaining a modest annual surplus driven by commodity exports. This period corresponds to the early BRI investment era and is consistent with the assessments of Lim et al. (2025) that Kazakhstan had retained meaningful trade autonomy. In the second phase, 2020--2021, a sharp pandemic-driven contraction in Chinese imports into Kazakhstan pushed imports down to US\$3.9 billion in 2020, while Kazakhstani raw material exports declined less severely, reflecting the relative inelasticity of Chinese industrial demand for commodity inputs compared to demand for consumer goods. In the third phase, 2022--2024, both export and import flows surged following the Ukraine invasion, but Chinese imports grew at a steeper rate than Kazakhstani exports, crossing the export line and generating a bilateral deficit for the first time in the series. The post-2022 surge in Chinese imports reflects three concurrent factors: rerouting away from the Northern Corridor, Chinese manufacturers filling market space vacated by sanctioned Russian and European suppliers, and the rapid expansion of Chinese automotive brands in the Kazakhstani consumer market (Figure~\ref{fig:iii1}).

\subsection{Sectoral Composition: The Commodity-Manufacture Divide}

The product composition of bilateral trade reveals a persistent divergence in economic complexity between what Kazakhstan exports and what it imports. In 2024, copper ore and refined copper together accounted for over US\$5.5 billion in Kazakhstani exports to China, and crude petroleum contributed US\$1.89 billion, with gold, radioactive chemicals, and ferrous metals comprising most of the remainder (Figure~\ref{fig:iii2a}). Kazakhstan's bilateral export revenues are therefore determined by global commodity prices and Chinese industrial demand conditions, neither of which Kazakhstani policymakers can influence directly. China's exports to Kazakhstan present a sharply contrasting profile: passenger vehicles (US\$1.05 billion in 2024), computers (US\$684 million), and telephones (US\$937 million) dominate, with Chinese brands including JAC Motors, Haval, and Chery displacing European and Japanese competitors as BRI logistics infrastructure reduced costs and the 2022 disruption eliminated key competing supply chains (Figure~\ref{fig:iii2b}). This configuration, with Kazakhstan supplying raw material inputs and China supplying value-added manufactured goods, is the commodity-manufacture divide that dependency and core-periphery frameworks identify as a structural marker of asymmetric exchange relations. The asymmetry operates at the level of economic complexity rather than trade volume alone.

The BRI investment decade has not generated revealed comparative advantage in higher-complexity products. Export growth across the period has been overwhelmingly intensive, meaning greater volume in existing commodity categories, rather than extensive, meaning entry into new product spaces.

\subsection{The Accelerating Deficit}

For most of the BRI era, Kazakhstan maintained a modest bilateral surplus with China (Table~\ref{tab:annual_trade}), which provided diplomatic cover for the win-win framing promoted by both governments and supported the Lim et al. (2025) assessment that dependency had been avoided. The structural basis of this surplus was commodity price-dependent rather than grounded in productive competitiveness. The unusually large surpluses recorded in 2020--2022 reflect pandemic-era suppression of Chinese consumer goods exports rather than any improvement in Kazakhstan's economic position. The structural break arrived in 2023, when Chinese imports into Kazakhstan surged 22.8\% and the bilateral balance reversed to a deficit of US\$4.03 billion while Kazakhstani export revenue growth stalled. The deficit narrowed to US\$0.51 billion in 2024 before widening to US\$1.8 billion in the first half of 2025 alone, driven by continued import growth in vehicles and electronics alongside a 10\% decline in commodity export revenues. The TAI series (Figure~\ref{fig:iii3b}) registers a sharp positive spike in 2023--2024 that is discontinuous with the near-zero values recorded from 2013 to 2019 and is inconsistent with a cyclical explanation, pointing instead to a structural change in the bilateral trade dynamic.

%% VII. COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS: CHINA, RUSSIA, AND THE EU

\section{Comparative Analysis: China, Russia, and the European Union}

\subsection{China versus Russia}

Kazakhstan's trade relationship with Russia is structured by the institutional architecture of the Eurasian Economic Union (EAEU), which establishes a common external tariff, harmonized regulatory standards, and a customs union framework. This regulatory convergence is a mutual constraint: Kazakhstan cannot unilaterally revise tariff schedules on Russian goods without implicating its EAEU commitments. The product composition of Russian trade with Kazakhstan is relatively diversified, encompassing agricultural goods, construction materials, processed foods, and light manufactures in addition to energy, reflecting decades of Soviet-era industrial integration. China's trade relationship with Kazakhstan operates under a different institutional logic. Bilateral customs facilitation agreements and BRI investment frameworks optimize transaction efficiency without producing the deep regulatory harmonization of EAEU membership, which preserves greater formal policy flexibility for Kazakhstan while simultaneously generating structural dependencies through investment concentration and market penetration. China's post-2022 position as the primary beneficiary of Northern Corridor disruption means it absorbed market share previously held by Russian exporters without taking on the corresponding political and regulatory obligations of EAEU partnership (Figure~\ref{fig:ii2b}).

\subsection{China versus the European Union}

Kazakhstan's EU trade relationship is the most technologically asymmetrical of its three primary partnerships and also the one most likely to generate productive upgrading incentives for Kazakhstani exporters. EU imports into Kazakhstan consist of precision machinery, capital goods, pharmaceutical products, and industrial equipment carrying significantly higher technological complexity than their Chinese counterparts. The EU's regulatory framework, including the emerging Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism, imposes an upgrading logic on Kazakhstani exporters that is absent from the China--Kazakhstan trade architecture: meeting EU certification requirements builds regulatory capacity with positive spillover effects. The investment comparison also distinguishes the two partnerships. Chinese FDI in Kazakhstan reached US\$11.4 billion by mid-2025, representing 32\% of China's total Central Asian investment, with the sectoral composition shifting over the study period as extractive industries fell from 68\% to 54\% of Chinese FDI stock while manufacturing rose from 13\% to 22\% and renewable energy from 4\% to 12\%. European FDI has been more concentrated in finance, services, and technology transfer under stronger transparency and governance requirements that generate institutional capacity spillovers. Kazakhstan has pursued a dual-track approach, accepting Chinese capital volumes while seeking EU standards alignment in key export sectors, but the outcomes of this strategy have to date favored Chinese commercial penetration over European-style productive upgrading (Figure~\ref{fig:iv2a}).

\subsection{Deindustrialization Risk and Societal Constraints}

Among Kazakhstan's three major trading partners, the Sino-Kazakh relationship generates the most acute combination of dependency risks because China's economic scale, competitive manufacturing position, and investment penetration operate simultaneously across multiple sectors rather than being confined to a single domain. The automotive sector illustrates this pattern concretely: Chinese brands have used Kazakhstan's EAEU membership to establish localization facilities that obtain ``Made in Kazakhstan'' certification, gaining tariff-free access to Russian and Central Asian markets. The strategic value of this arrangement accrues primarily to Chinese manufacturers gaining EAEU market access, while Kazakhstan's position in the value chain is confined to lower-complexity assembly operations. Rather than building an independent supply chain, Kazakhstan has become an institutional gateway for Chinese industrial expansion into the broader EAEU market. These structural risks are compounded by domestic political constraints. The pattern of high-level diplomatic harmony coexisting with persistent popular skepticism toward Chinese economic activity has repeatedly constrained the government's room for maneuver. The 2016 land reform protests, sustained opposition to the ``55 Factories'' plan, and public anger over the treatment of ethnic Kazakhs in Xinjiang have created a domestic environment in which the government must defend Chinese economic engagement without being able to address the commodity-dependence and labor-market concerns that motivate public opposition. Neither administrative restriction nor strategic communications campaigns has resolved the underlying structural grievances, and these constraints are likely to intensify as the trade deficit deepens and the visibility of Chinese commercial activity in domestic consumer markets increases (Primiano \& Kudebayeva, 2025; MERICS, 2022).

%% VIII. SYNTHESIS AND CONCLUSIONS
%% ============================================================
\section{Synthesis and Conclusions}

The empirical record assembled across the preceding sections supports a consistent finding. Between 2015 and 2025, Kazakhstan--China economic relations moved from a condition of relative bilateral symmetry to one of accelerating structural asymmetry. The Trade Intensity Index has risen continuously over the period, indicating that bilateral trade is now more concentrated relative to Kazakhstan's global trade position than market-share fundamentals would predict. The RCA analysis shows no meaningful shift beyond commodity categories across a decade of BRI investment. The Trade Asymmetry Index registers a discontinuous positive spike from 2023 onward that cannot be attributed to cyclical commodity price variation and is consistent with a structural break in the bilateral trade dynamic following the post-2022 geopolitical reconfiguration of Eurasian trade flows. The bilateral trade deficit, absent before 2023, reached US\$1.8 billion in the first half of 2025.

These findings bear on the theoretical debate between complex interdependence and asymmetric dependence, and they suggest that the two frameworks are not mutually exclusive but operate at different levels of the same relationship. Keohane and Nye's framework correctly diagnoses the structural condition: the Sino-Kazakh relationship exhibits all three defining characteristics of complex interdependence. Multiple channels---energy pipelines, BRI investment flows, the Trans-Caspian transit corridor, and consumer goods supply chains---connect the two economies simultaneously, and the costs of exit across all channels at once are prohibitive. The absence of a fixed issue hierarchy is confirmed by the qualitative evidence: trade facilitation and logistics access have displaced sovereignty protection as the operational priority in bilateral negotiations, as demonstrated by the automotive roadmap's conversion from a diversification instrument to a Chinese EAEU market-access vehicle. The diminished utility of military force is structurally embedded---Kazakhstan has no military instrument capable of altering the commodity-manufacture terms of bilateral exchange.

The sensitivity--vulnerability distinction, however, is where the data generate the most precise theoretical finding. Through 2022, Kazakhstan exhibited high sensitivity to Chinese demand conditions---commodity price cycles and Chinese industrial output fluctuations transmitted rapidly into bilateral trade balances---but retained residual vulnerability-reduction capacity through the multivector diplomatic portfolio. The 2023--2024 TAI data mark the point at which this residual capacity was exhausted: the 369\% import surge established a new structural floor that has not reversed, and Kazakhstan has not demonstrably adjusted its export composition or reduced its import exposure in the subsequent period. This is the empirical signature of a transition from sensitivity to vulnerability in Keohane and Nye's terms. The relationship has crossed the threshold at which Kazakhstan's interdependence cost is no longer transient and policy-adjustable but durable and structurally embedded.

Hirschman's asymmetric dependence thesis, rather than contradicting complex interdependence, identifies the mechanism that explains why this vulnerability threshold was reached. Kazakhstan's alternatives have not expanded proportionally with China's growing share, and the commodity-manufacture divide ensures that the terms of bilateral exchange are structurally deteriorating from Kazakhstan's perspective regardless of the diplomatic activity maintained at the elite level. The instruments deployed---BRI co-investment declarations, automotive localization roadmaps, Middle Corridor expansion agreements---have not demonstrably altered the product composition of bilateral trade. They have functioned as transaction-level facilitation instruments that deepened the multiple channels of the relationship without generating the productive diversification that could convert Kazakhstan's sensitivity into manageable rather than embedded vulnerability. The theoretical conclusion is that complex interdependence correctly describes the architecture of the relationship while Hirschman correctly predicts its directional trajectory: high-channel-density interdependence becomes a structural trap when the smaller state lacks the productive upgrading capacity to expand its alternatives at a rate commensurate with the dominant partner's growing share.

Kazakhstan's multi-vector foreign policy doctrine remains institutionally intact in formal diplomatic terms, but it faces its most significant structural test of the post-independence period. Russia's strategic weakening after 2022 has not diversified Kazakhstan's economic relationships; it has concentrated them further around China, as Chinese manufacturers absorbed market share from both Russian suppliers and Western alternatives simultaneously. Sustaining multivectorism as a substantive commitment rather than a diplomatic posture requires at minimum an industrial policy targeting the extensive export margin, that is, entry into higher-complexity product categories where Kazakhstan can develop genuine comparative advantage rather than simply increasing volume in existing commodity sectors.

For other small and medium states navigating commodity-dependent integration with a dominant industrial partner, the Kazakhstan case illustrates a dynamic that aggregate trade figures can obscure during periods of high commodity prices. Structural asymmetry becomes visible when price cycles turn and import penetration accelerates at the same time. Diplomatic and institutional mechanisms are a necessary but not sufficient response. Without productive diversification that shifts the export structure toward higher-complexity goods, smaller states remain exposed to the terms-of-trade deterioration that the Hirschman thesis predicts, regardless of the sophistication of their hedging strategies.
