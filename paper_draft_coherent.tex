\begin{abstract}
This paper synthesizes the project evidence on Kazakhstan's external trade structure and the post-2022 acceleration of trade integration with China. Using existing WTO-based project outputs for 2015--2024, we compare China with Russia and the EU, examine bilateral composition and sectoral asymmetries, and assess the shift from sustained bilateral surpluses to deficits. The evidence indicates that China has moved from a secondary to a co-leading trade partner in Kazakhstan's comparison set, while trade structure remains asymmetric: Kazakhstan exports commodities and semi-processed materials, but imports increasingly concentrated manufactured goods. The 2023 break in the bilateral balance and high sectoral import concentration imply persistent vulnerability unless diversification and domestic upgrading keep pace with trade expansion.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Kazakhstan's external economic strategy is formally multivector, yet the composition of its trade has changed substantially over the last decade. This paper consolidates project outputs into a single narrative with consistent interpretation from Kazakhstan's perspective.

Unless explicitly stated otherwise, all bilateral statistics are interpreted as follows: exports are goods Kazakhstan sells to China, and imports are goods Kazakhstan buys from China.

\section{Data and Scope}
The analysis uses existing project tables and figures generated from WTO-oriented bilateral data workflows. The effective annual window in the existing outputs is 2015--2024. No new data are introduced in this manuscript.

\subsection{Statistical Discrepancies and Data Politics}
A recurring interpretation issue is denominator choice: partner shares computed in a restricted comparison set can imply stronger apparent concentration than shares measured against full world trade totals. The project's multivector and asymmetry figures illustrate this distinction.

\subsection{China vs EU}
The existing project narrative treats China and the EU as functionally different channels: China for high-volume manufactured supply and the EU for standards-intensive links and high-value export destinations.

\subsection{Perceived Risks}
Dependency concerns are tied to three interacting patterns visible in existing outputs: import concentration in key sectors, commodity-heavy export profile, and post-2023 balance deterioration. These patterns reinforce deindustrialization concerns unless offset by domestic upgrading and supplier diversification.

\section{Conclusion}
Using only existing project outputs, the evidence supports four conclusions. First, 2023 is a structural inflection point in bilateral trade balance dynamics. Second, China's role in Kazakhstan's trade structure has deepened materially in the late period. Third, the relationship remains compositionally asymmetric (commodity exports versus manufactured imports). Fourth, vulnerability is concentrated in high-penetration sectors, implying that diversification and industrial upgrading are necessary for preserving multivector policy autonomy.
